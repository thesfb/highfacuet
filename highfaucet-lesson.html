Chapter 1 — Foundations · Lesson 3 of 5

Completeness
& Soundness

Every cryptographic proof system rests on two properties. Understanding them precisely — not just vaguely — is the foundation everything else is built on.

The two pillars

A proof system involves two parties: a prover who wants to convince someone of a statement, and a verifier who decides whether to believe them. For this to be meaningful, the system must satisfy two guarantees simultaneously.

Without both properties holding, the proof system breaks down — either honest provers get rejected for no reason, or dishonest provers can fabricate acceptance. Neither is acceptable.

Definition — Completeness

If a statement is true and the prover is honest, then an honest verifier will always accept the proof. A complete system never rejects a valid proof.

Definition — Soundness

If a statement is false, then no prover — even a computationally unbounded cheater — can convince an honest verifier to accept it, except with negligible probability ε.

Formalising it

Let L be a language — the set of all true statements. A prover P supplies a witness w alongside the statement x. The verifier V runs a verification algorithm and outputs accept or reject.

Formal definitions
// Completeness — honest prover always accepted
∀ x ∈ L, ∀ valid witness w:
  Pr[ V(x, P(x, w)) = accept ] = 1

// Soundness — no cheating prover can fake it
∀ x ∉ L, ∀ adversarial P*:
  Pr[ V(x, P*(x)) = accept ] ≤ ε (negligible)

The soundness error ε is the maximum probability a cheating prover can fool the verifier. In practice, we want this to be cryptographically negligible — something like 2⁻¹²⁸. If the protocol is run multiple times, repeated rounds can reduce ε exponentially.

Statistical vs computational soundness

There are two flavours of soundness, and they're not the same:

Statistical soundness holds even against a prover with unlimited computation. The cheating probability is bounded by the information-theoretic structure of the protocol itself. This is the stronger guarantee.

Computational soundness holds only against a prover bounded by polynomial time. A computationally unbounded adversary might break it. Most SNARKs in practice provide computational soundness based on hardness assumptions.

→

STARKs aim for statistical soundness (and post-quantum security) by avoiding elliptic curve pairings. SNARKs like Groth16 achieve computational soundness — relying on the hardness of the discrete log problem. You'll see this tradeoff throughout the course.

Why this matters for ZK

In zero-knowledge proofs, we add a third property on top of completeness and soundness: that the proof reveals nothing beyond the truth of the statement. But before you can reason about what a proof leaks, you need to be crystal clear about what it proves.

Completeness and soundness define the semantics of the proof. The zero-knowledge property defines its privacy. Neither means anything without the other.

Question 01 / 02
Which property guarantees that a cheating prover cannot convince the verifier of a false statement?
Correct. Soundness is the property that prevents forgery. Completeness is the mirror: it ensures honest provers are never wrongly rejected. The two work together — soundness without completeness would reject everyone; completeness without soundness would accept anyone.
Question 02 / 02
A proof system has computational soundness. What does this mean?
Correct. Computational soundness is conditional — it relies on hardness assumptions (like discrete log). A computationally unbounded adversary could in theory break it. Statistical soundness, by contrast, holds unconditionally. STARKs target statistical soundness; most SNARKs settle for computational soundness in exchange for shorter proofs.
